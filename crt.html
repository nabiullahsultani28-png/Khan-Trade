The CRT Model

Candle Range Theory, explained.

A higher-timeframe candle draws a range. Price accumulates inside it, sweeps the liquidity resting just beyond it, then expands to the other side. That's CRT — a complete entry model, not a vague concept. Here's exactly how it works, and how to actually get results from it.

How it works Live example Getting results
The idea in one minute

What is Candle Range Theory?

A higher-timeframe candle's high and low draw a range — the CRH and CRL. Inside it, price runs the same three moves every session. Here it is on Gold:

Example · XAUUSD Daily range 2,640 CRL → 2,680 CRH
  1. 1

    Accumulation wait

    Price coils between 2,655–2,675 inside the range. Boring on purpose. Nothing to do.

  2. 2

    Manipulation the trap

    London spikes to 2,686 — just above the CRH — grabbing breakout buyers' stops, then snaps back inside. That's the sweep.

  3. 3

    Distribution the trade

    Rejected, price expands down toward 2,640 and beyond. You short the high, stop above the wick, target the low.

Same three moves, every session — flip it for buys. Learn it on one candle and entry, stop and target are all drawn by the range itself.

The vocabulary

CRH
Candle Range High — top of the range. Liquidity sits just above it.
CRL
Candle Range Low — bottom of the range. Liquidity sits just below it.
Sweep
A wick that pierces CRH or CRL to grab stops, then closes back inside — the trap.
Premium
Upper half of the range — where you look to sell after a high sweep.
Discount
Lower half of the range — where you look to buy after a low sweep.
Expansion
The drive from the swept edge to the opposite edge — your target.
Anatomy of a range

One range, three phases.

Accumulate inside the range, sweep the high to trap breakout buyers, then expand down through the discount toward the opposite edge. This is a sell-side example — flip it for buys.

How to trade it

From range to entry, step by step.

CRT is a checklist, not a feeling. Every trade is the same four moves — and if any step is missing, there's no trade. That's what makes it teachable and repeatable.

01

Mark the range

Take the high and low of your reference candle (weekly/daily). Draw the CRH and CRL — that's your map for the session.

02

Wait for the sweep

Do nothing until price runs liquidity beyond CRH or CRL. No sweep, no setup — the sweep is the signal, not the breakout.

03

Confirm the reversal

Drop to a lower timeframe and wait for a market-structure shift back inside the range — a clean rejection that confirms the sweep was a trap.

04

Target the opposite edge

Enter on the confirmation, stop just beyond the swept wick, and target the other side of the range (the expansion). Defined risk, defined reward.

Timing

When CRT fires

CRT is a session model. The sweep and expansion almost always happen inside the London and New York kill-zones, when real liquidity enters the market. Outside those windows there's usually nothing to do but study.

The edge

Why it works

Breakout traders pile stops just beyond obvious highs and lows. CRT positions you on the other side of that crowd — entering exactly where their stops get hunted, with the expansion in your favour.

How you actually get results

The model is the easy part.

Knowing CRT and profiting from CRT are two different skills. Results don't come from finding more setups — they come from how you manage risk, expectancy, and yourself across a large sample of trades.

Define risk first

Decide what one trade is allowed to lose before you enter — a fixed fraction of the account per trade (your "1R"). The CRT stop sits just beyond the swept wick, so risk is defined by the chart, not by hope.

Let expectancy compound

Because the target is the opposite edge of the range, clean CRT trades routinely pay 1:3+ R. At that reward-to-risk you don't need to be right often — a modest win rate across a big sample still grows the account. Think in R, not in dollars.

Journal every range

Screenshot each CRH/CRL, whether the sweep and confirmation appeared, and the outcome in R. Over 50–100 trades the data — not your memory — tells you which ranges and sessions are your real edge.

The honest part: CRT removes guesswork from where to trade — it can't remove drawdowns, losing streaks, or the discipline to wait. Results are a function of repeating a defined process over a large sample, not of any single trade. Everything here is educational, not financial advice.
