ICT Concepts

The ICT framework, decoded.

Inner Circle Trader concepts strip the market down to one idea: price is engineered to take liquidity and rebalance inefficiency. Master the handful of building blocks below — liquidity, order blocks, fair value gaps and market structure — and the chart stops looking random. This is the foundation every CRT and Unicorn setup is built on.

The idea in one minute

What is ICT?

Retail traders chase breakouts; ICT tracks the two things big money cares about: where the liquidity is, and where price is mispriced. Everything reduces to one repeating sequence.

1. Liquidity

Stop orders pile up above highs and below lows. This resting liquidity is the fuel price is drawn to.

2. The Raid

Price spikes through a high or low to sweep the liquidity, trapping breakout traders. The trap is the setup.

3. Displacement

A sharp, one-sided move breaks structure, leaving an inefficiency (Fair Value Gap) behind.

4. The Return

Price retraces to the FVG or Order Block that caused the displacement. That retrace is your entry.

Anatomy of an ICT move

Liquidity, displacement, return.

Price sweeps the sell-side liquidity below the low, displaces up to break structure, leaves a Fair Value Gap, then retraces into the order block / FVG before expanding to the buy-side liquidity above. This is a long example — flip it for shorts.

The building blocks

Six concepts do the heavy lifting.

Every ICT setup — including CRT and the Unicorn — is assembled from these pieces. Learn what each one is and why it matters, and the rest is just sequencing them.

Market structure

Higher highs and higher lows = bullish; the reverse = bearish. A Break of Structure continues the trend; a Market Structure Shift is the first sign it's flipping. Structure tells you which side to trade.

Liquidity (BSL / SSL)

Stops cluster above equal highs and below equal lows. That resting liquidity is the magnet — price is engineered to reach it before it reverses. Find the liquidity and you've found price's next destination.

Order blocks

The last down-candle before an up-move (or up-candle before a down-move) marks where institutions loaded their position. Price respects these blocks on the return — a precise, repeatable entry zone with a tight stop.

Fair Value Gaps

A three-candle imbalance left by fast, one-sided movement — a gap the market skipped over. Price tends to return and "rebalance" it. FVGs are both entries on the retrace and targets on the expansion.

Premium & discount

Split any range at its midpoint (equilibrium). You sell from the premium half and buy from the discount half — entering at a price institutions would consider fair value, never chasing.

Kill zones (timing)

ICT setups fire on a schedule — the London and New York sessions, when real volume enters. The same pattern outside those windows is noise. Timing is the filter that turns a concept into a trade.

How a trade forms

From bias to entry, in four moves.

ICT isn't a feeling — it's a sequence. Each setup is the same four steps, and if a step is missing there's no trade. That's what makes it teachable instead of guesswork.

01

Set the bias

On a higher timeframe, mark the dealing range and split it premium/discount. Decide which liquidity is the draw — are we reaching for highs above, or lows below?

02

Mark the liquidity

Identify the obvious highs and lows where stops rest (BSL/SSL), plus the order blocks and FVGs in between. That's your map of where price wants to go and where it'll react.

03

Wait for the sweep + shift

Do nothing until price raids a liquidity pool and then displaces the other way, breaking structure (MSS). The sweep is the trap; the displacement is the confirmation.

04

Enter on the return

Enter as price retraces into the order block or FVG that caused the displacement. Stop beyond the swept extreme, target the opposite liquidity. Defined risk, defined reward.

Timing

When ICT fires

The sweep-and-displace sequence almost always lands inside the London and New York kill-zones, when institutional liquidity enters. Outside those windows price drifts — there's usually nothing to do but mark levels and wait.

The edge

Why it works

Breakout traders stack stops at obvious highs and lows. ICT puts you on the other side of that crowd — entering exactly where their stops are hunted, with displacement and the next liquidity pool working in your favour.

The same building blocks marked up on a real chart. Watch the liquidity get swept, the displacement break structure and leave a fair value gap, then price return to the order block before expanding — exactly the sequence above.

Live example
  • BSL / SSL the resting liquidity above the highs and below the lows that price hunts
  • Sweep + MSS the raid on that liquidity, followed by the displacement that breaks structure
  • OB / FVG the order block and fair value gap left behind — your entry on the return
  • Expansion the drive toward the opposite liquidity — the move you're paid for
